For GROW Clients

GROW clients are typically people leaders seeking to strengthen leadership skills, navigate team dynamics, and prepare for broader impact. Early sessions should balance immediate goals with deeper reflection.

  • Acknowledge their dual role as both individual contributors and team leaders
  • Review topics from their welcome email
  • Ask: "What's one leadership behavior you'd like to strengthen in the next quarter?"
  • Ask: "How do you want your team to describe your leadership style six months from now?"
  • Gently explore pressure points around change management or influence

For EXEC Clients

EXEC clients are seasoned leaders focused on strategic vision, organizational influence, and high-stakes decisions. Sessions should anchor in both tactical and visionary thinking.

  • Start with: "What's one strategic priority you're navigating right now?"
  • Review their welcome email topics and familiarize yourself with their goals
  • Encourage reflection on leadership identity, legacy, and enterprise-wide impact
  • Frame the partnership clearly: "I'm here as a thinking partner‚Äîyou bring the agenda, and I'll help you sharpen and stretch it."

The GROW Model
Goal setting ¬∑ Problem solving ¬∑ Coaching structure
Beginner-Friendly

The GROW Model is a structured coaching and problem-solving framework that helps individuals set goals, explore realities, generate options, and commit to action. Developed by Sir John Whitmore, it fosters clarity, accountability, and progress in personal and professional development.

The Four Stages

StageFocusExample Questions
GoalWhat do you want?"What do you want to achieve? What does success look like? How will you measure progress?"
RealityWhere are you now?"What is happening right now? What challenges are you facing? What strengths can you leverage?"
OptionsWhat could you do?"What are all the possible ways forward? What has worked in similar situations? What resources can help?"
Way ForwardWhat will you do?"What actions will you take? What obstacles might arise? How will you track progress?"

Real-World Use Cases

Client ChallengeHow GROW Helps
"I don't know where to start with my goal."Clarifies outcomes and direction (Goal)
"I feel stuck or unclear on my progress."Unpacks what's really happening now (Reality)
"I don't know what my options are."Generates creative solutions and strategies (Options)
"I set goals but don't follow through."Builds commitment and action plans (Way Forward)
"I avoid accountability conversations."Offers a nonjudgmental, empowering structure for dialogue

Session Template

üìã GROW Coaching Template

Goal: What do you want to achieve? What would success look like?

Reality: What is happening right now? What's helping or hindering you? What have you already tried?

Options: What could you do? What else? And what else? What are the pros/cons of each option?

Way Forward: What will you do first? When will you do it? What support or resources do you need?

  • "What's getting in your way right now?"
  • "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"

Why Use the GROW Model

  • Clarity: Breaks down goals into manageable steps
  • Self-Discovery: Encourages clients to find their own solutions
  • Accountability: Transforms ideas into committed action
  • Adaptability: Works in leadership, coaching, and personal development

The SCARF Model
Team dynamics ¬∑ Psychological safety ¬∑ Motivation
Intermediate

The SCARF Model, developed by David Rock of the NeuroLeadership Institute, explains how social interactions trigger the same reward and threat responses in the brain as physical survival instincts. Understanding SCARF helps leaders and coaches reduce social threats and enhance collaboration, motivation, and engagement.

The Five Domains

DomainThreat ResponseReward Response
StatusFeeling undervalued or compared negatively to othersRecognition, respect, and appreciation
CertaintyUnclear expectations or unpredictable changesClear goals, structure, and transparency
AutonomyMicromanagement or lack of decision-making powerFreedom to make choices and control one's work
RelatednessFeeling isolated or excluded from the groupTrust, connection, and belonging
FairnessPerceived favoritism or unfair treatmentOpen, equitable decision-making processes

How to Apply It for Growth & Leadership

  • Status: Recognize and celebrate achievements; provide opportunities for professional development
  • Certainty: Communicate clear expectations; keep people informed about organizational changes
  • Autonomy: Empower with decision-making authority; encourage flexibility and ownership
  • Relatedness: Foster an inclusive environment; encourage team bonding and collaboration
  • Fairness: Maintain transparency in policies; apply rules consistently to all team members

Real-World Use Cases

Client ChallengeSCARF Domain
"I feel micromanaged."Autonomy threat
"This change feels sudden and unclear."Certainty gap
"I don't feel recognized for my contributions."Status threat
"I feel like an outsider on this team."Relatedness threat
"The rules seem to apply differently to different people."Fairness threat

Coaching Prompts

  • "Which of the five SCARF domains feels most threatened for you right now?"
  • "What would increase your sense of certainty in this situation?"
  • "How might your team members be experiencing this change through SCARF?"
  • "Where do you feel the most autonomy in your work ‚Äî and where do you feel the least?"
  • "What would need to be true for this situation to feel fair to you?"

Ikigai

Beginner-Friendly

Ikigai (Áîü„Åç„Åå„ÅÑ) is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." Individuals find deep purpose, motivation, and fulfillment when four areas align: what they love, what they're good at, what the world needs, and what they can be paid for.

The Four Intersections

  • What you love ‚Äî Passion: activities, causes, and interests that energize you
  • What you're good at ‚Äî Vocation: skills, strengths, and natural talents
  • What the world needs ‚Äî Mission: problems, opportunities, and communities you care about
  • What you can be paid for ‚Äî Profession: roles, services, or markets where your skills have value

How to Use It in a Session

  • Identifying Gaps: If a client enjoys something but isn't paid for it, discuss ways to monetize it. If they are skilled but lack passion, explore ways to rekindle interest or pivot their skills toward meaningful work.
  • Career & Life Decisions: Use Ikigai to help clients navigate career transitions, entrepreneurship, or retirement. Assist in setting aligned goals that balance fulfillment with professional success.
  • Building Action Plans: Encourage small, actionable steps to bring more Ikigai into daily life. Integrate purpose-driven practices like gratitude, mindfulness, and continuous learning.

Intermediate

The Johari Window is a psychological model developed by Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham (1955) that helps individuals and teams enhance self-awareness, improve communication, and foster stronger interpersonal relationships. It categorizes knowledge about oneself into four quadrants based on what is known to self and to others.

The Four Quadrants

Open Area
(Known to self & others)		Behaviors, feelings, and skills openly shared. Encourages transparency, trust, and collaboration.Expand this area through openness
Blind Spot
(Unknown to self, known to others)		Traits or behaviors others see that the individual is unaware of. Constructive feedback helps uncover these.Seek and receive feedback
Hidden Area
(Known to self, unknown to others)		Private thoughts, experiences, and emotions not shared with others. Reducing this through disclosure fosters deeper connections.Build trust, increase self-disclosure
Unknown Area
(Unknown to self & others)		Untapped potential, unconscious behaviors, or suppressed experiences. New experiences and reflection can reveal hidden strengths.Explore through new experiences

How to Use It in a Session

  • Seek Feedback: Ask trusted colleagues, mentors, or coaches to share honest insights about strengths and areas for growth
  • Increase Self-Disclosure: Share thoughts or concerns with others to build trust and improve relationships
  • Engage in Self-Reflection: Regularly assess behaviors and reactions through journaling or mindfulness
  • Embrace New Experiences: Step outside the comfort zone to discover hidden talents and expand self-awareness

The Ladder of Inference
Decision-making ¬∑ Assumptions ¬∑ Critical thinking
Advanced

Developed by Chris Argyris, the Ladder of Inference illustrates how people move from observing reality to making decisions and taking actions ‚Äî often without realizing the assumptions and biases involved. By understanding this process, individuals and teams can improve decision-making, reduce miscommunication, and challenge automatic thinking patterns.

The Rungs (Bottom to Top)

1
Reality & Facts: The objective data and events happening around us. Example: A team member submits a report late.
2
Selected Data: We focus on certain details while ignoring others (often unconsciously). Example: We notice the delay but overlook that they were handling multiple priorities.
3
Interpreted Meaning: We assign meaning based on past experiences. Example: "They are unreliable when it comes to deadlines."
4
Assumptions: We make assumptions based on our interpretation. Example: "They don't take deadlines seriously."
5
Conclusions: We form a belief based on our assumptions. Example: "I can't count on them for important projects."
6
Beliefs: Our conclusions reinforce existing beliefs and influence future thinking. Example: "They're not a strong performer."
7
Actions: We act based on our beliefs, often without questioning how we got there. Example: We stop assigning them important tasks.

How to Slow Down the Ladder

  • Pause and Reflect: Ask ‚Äî am I reacting based on facts or assumptions?
  • Expand Data Selection: Seek additional perspectives before drawing conclusions
  • Test Assumptions: Ask ‚Äî is my assumption accurate? What evidence supports it?
  • Stay Open: Engage in dialogue before making judgments

Real-World Use Cases

Client ChallengeHow the Ladder of Inference Helps
"I jumped to conclusions about a colleague's behavior."Helps identify where on the ladder the leap happened
"I made a decision I later regretted."Reveals the assumption or interpretation that drove the choice
"I feel like I'm always right but others disagree."Exposes personal data filters and belief systems
"There's a breakdown in trust with someone on my team."Uncovers unspoken assumptions on both sides

Coaching Prompts

  • "What did you actually observe ‚Äî just the facts, without interpretation?"
  • "What meaning did you assign to that? Where does that come from?"
  • "What might you be missing by focusing on this data?"
  • "What would someone else conclude from the same situation?"
  • "What would it take to go back down the ladder and check your assumptions?"

Mind Mapping
Creative thinking ¬∑ Problem solving ¬∑ Idea organization
Beginner-Friendly

Popularized by Tony Buzan in the 1970s, mind mapping is a visual brainstorming technique used to capture, organize, and explore ideas. By starting with a central concept and branching out into related ideas, mind maps help structure thoughts, enhance creativity, and improve memory retention.

How to Create a Mind Map

1
Start with the Central Idea: Write the main topic or challenge in the center of the page
2
Create Main Branches: Draw lines outward, labeling each with a key subtopic or category
3
Add Sub-Branches: Expand on each branch with additional details, insights, or related concepts. Use keywords, not long sentences.
4
Use Colors and Symbols: Differentiate ideas using colors and icons to improve visual recall and engagement
5
Refine and Connect: Look for patterns and relationships between concepts. Link related ideas with arrows.

When to Use Mind Mapping

  • Brainstorming ideas and solutions creatively
  • Organizing complex information visually
  • Planning projects, presentations, or reports
  • Exploring a coaching topic from multiple angles
  • Making sense of a messy, overwhelming situation

Real-World Use Cases

The Power of Habit Loop
Behavior change ¬∑ Routines ¬∑ Productivity
Beginner-Friendly

The Habit Loop, introduced by Charles Duhigg in The Power of Habit (2012), explains how habits form and can be changed. It consists of three key components: Cue ‚Üí Routine ‚Üí Reward. Understanding this cycle allows clients to modify behaviors and replace unproductive habits with beneficial ones.

The Three Components

  • Cue (Trigger): The event or signal that initiates a habit ‚Äî could be a time, location, emotion, person, or preceding action
  • Routine: The behavior or action taken in response to the cue ‚Äî mental, physical, or emotional
  • Reward: The benefit or satisfaction received, which reinforces the habit loop

How to Use It in a Session

1
Identify the Habit Loop: Break down a habit into its three components. Ask: What triggers this habit? What action follows automatically? What reward is reinforcing the behavior? Encourage clients to track patterns over a few days.
2
Disrupt Unwanted Habits: Modify the routine while keeping the same cue and reward. Example: If stress triggers mindless scrolling (routine) for relaxation (reward), replace scrolling with deep breathing ‚Äî same reward, healthier routine.
3
Build New Positive Habits: Attach new habits to existing cues (habit stacking). Ensure the reward is satisfying. Start small and build consistency.
4
Reinforce & Track Progress: Celebrate small wins and adjust as needed. Regular reflection on habit success and obstacles ensures long-term sustainability.

Real-World Use Cases

Client ChallengeHow Habit Loop Helps
"I keep procrastinating on hard tasks."Identifies cue (discomfort), routine (avoidance), and reward (relief) to design a better response
"I start my day with good intentions but lose focus."Audits morning habits and creates a routine that aligns with values
"I want to build healthier communication habits."Unpacks social cues and emotional responses to replace reactions with intentional behaviors
"I want to meditate/work out/write, but I can't stick with it."Uses habit stacking and reward reinforcement to lock in new behavior
"I check email or Slack obsessively."Reveals unconscious habit loops driven by distraction and dopamine

Step 4 ‚Äî Plan a test run: When will you try this? For how long? How will you celebrate or track success?

  • "When does this habit usually happen? What comes right before it?"
  • "How do you feel before, during, and after?"
  • "What small shift could you try instead?"

The Three-Brain Model
Decision-making ¬∑ Intuition ¬∑ Whole-body awareness
Intermediate

The Three-Brain Model suggests that humans have three interconnected intelligence centers ‚Äî Head, Heart, and Gut ‚Äî each playing a distinct role in decision-making, intuition, and behavior. Understanding and integrating these three "brains" helps individuals make more balanced, insightful, and authentic choices in leadership and life.

The Three Intelligence Centers

CenterFunctionsOveruse Risk
Head (Cognitive)Analysis, reasoning, planning, problem-solving, languageOverthinking, indecision, ignoring emotions and intuition

Also known as the Urgent-Important Matrix, the Eisenhower Matrix helps clients prioritize tasks based on urgency and importance. Inspired by President Dwight D. The Four Quadrants

QuadrantActionDescription
Q1: Urgent + ImportantDo FirstTasks that require immediate attention and have significant consequences if delayed ‚Äî crises, deadlines, pressing problems
Q2: Important, Not UrgentScheduleTasks that contribute to long-term goals but don't require immediate action ‚Äî strategic planning, skill development, relationship-building
Q3: Urgent, Not ImportantDelegateTasks that need to be done quickly but can be handled by someone else ‚Äî interruptions, routine requests, low-value meetings
Q4: NeitherEliminateDistractions or time-wasters that don't contribute to goals ‚Äî busywork, excessive social media, time-wasters

3
Reduce Q3 & Q4: Guide clients in delegating non-essential urgent tasks (Q3). Encourage eliminating Q4 distractions.
4

The 5 Whys
Root cause analysis ¬∑ Problem-solving ¬∑ Continuous improvement
Beginner-Friendly

The 5 Whys is a simple yet powerful root cause analysis technique developed by Sakichi Toyoda and adopted by Toyota as part of its Lean manufacturing practices. By repeatedly asking "Why?" ‚Äî typically five times ‚Äî individuals and teams can move beyond surface-level symptoms and uncover the fundamental issue.

How to Use It

1
Identify the Problem: Clearly define the issue. Be specific and concise.
2
Ask "Why?" (1st): Why did the problem occur? Record the answer.
3
Ask "Why?" (2nd‚Äì5th): Take each answer and ask why that happened. Continue until you reach the root cause ‚Äî often by the 5th iteration, sometimes more or fewer.
4
Implement Corrective Action: Once the root cause is identified, develop actions to address it and prevent recurrence.

Example in Action

üìã 5 Whys Example: Missed Project Deadline

Problem: The team missed an important project deadline.

Why 1? The final report wasn't ready in time.
Why 2? Data analysis took longer than expected.
Why 3? The data was incomplete and required rework.
Why 4? Data collection was inconsistent.
Why 5? There were no standardized data collection procedures.

Root Cause: Lack of a standardized process.
Solution: Develop and implement a data collection protocol.

"Our team keeps missing goals."Identifies systemic gaps in process or communication
"I keep having the same conflict with a colleague."Surfaces unspoken needs or recurring patterns

Cancellation & Late Cancel Policy
12-hour window ¬∑ Self-pay ¬∑ Coach compensation
Policy
Quick Reference
Late cancel = canceled within 12 hours of scheduled time ¬∑ Coaches are compensated for late cancels ¬∑ Late cancels count toward the client's session allocation ¬∑ Self-pay sessions: $80/session

What Counts as a Late Cancellation?

Any session canceled within 12 hours of the scheduled start time. Coaches are under no obligation to accept reschedule requests within the 12-hour window. If you choose to reschedule, that's at your discretion.

How to Handle a Late Cancel

1
You'll receive a cancellation email. The session should auto-label as "Late Cancel" in the Coach Portal.
2
If a client emails you to cancel within the window, log in manually and mark it as "Late Cancel" in the Coach Portal to ensure compensation.
3
Consider reaching out to check in with the client and maintain the relationship.

Guidance: Discussing Session Length & Frequency
How to handle client requests for longer or more frequent sessions
Guidance

Key Principles

  • Present options, not recommendations. Frame longer or more frequent sessions as options, not prescriptions. Example: "Boon offers options for longer or more frequent sessions, which can be paid for by the client or approved by their HR team."
  • Avoid "up-sell" language. Keep tone neutral. Instead of "I highly recommend a 45-minute session," say: "Some clients find longer sessions helpful for in-depth discussions ‚Äî this might be something you'd like to consider."
  • Check approval requirements. HR must provide written approval for changes to the standard arrangement. Have HR email approval to [email protected].
  • Recommend 12 sessions. Please recommend scheduling 12 sessions for deeper engagement and a more seamless coaching journey.

When to Refer: Signs That Go Beyond Coaching
Symptoms that indicate a need for mental health referral
Clinical Guidance
Important Note
The appearance of any one symptom alone (except for active suicidality) does not necessitate immediate referral. When several symptoms appear together and the client is not presenting as their typical self, a referral must be made.

Symptoms to watch for include: marked changes in mood or behavior, expressions of hopelessness, references to self-harm, significant withdrawal from relationships or activities, paranoia or disconnection from reality, and inability to function in daily life.

Exception: Active suicidal ideation or any life-threatening situation requires immediate action regardless of other symptoms. See the Emergency Protocol above.
