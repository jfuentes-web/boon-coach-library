Session #1 Coaching Tips (by Client Type)
Tailored guidance for GROW and EXEC clients
Reference

For GROW Clients

GROW clients are typically people leaders seeking to strengthen leadership skills, navigate team dynamics, and prepare for broader impact. Early sessions should balance immediate goals with deeper reflection.

  • Acknowledge their dual role as both individual contributors and team leaders
  • Review topics from their welcome email
  • Ask: "What's one leadership behavior you'd like to strengthen in the next quarter?"
  • Ask: "How do you want your team to describe your leadership style six months from now?"
  • Gently explore pressure points around change management or influence

For EXEC Clients

EXEC clients are seasoned leaders focused on strategic vision, organizational influence, and high-stakes decisions. Sessions should anchor in both tactical and visionary thinking.

  • Start with: "What's one strategic priority you're navigating right now?"
  • Review their welcome email topics and familiarize yourself with their goals
  • Encourage reflection on leadership identity, legacy, and enterprise-wide impact
  • Frame the partnership clearly: "I'm here as a thinking partner‚Äîyou bring the agenda, and I'll help you sharpen and stretch it."

The GROW Model
Goal setting ¬∑ Problem solving ¬∑ Coaching structure
Beginner-Friendly

A structured coaching framework that helps individuals set goals, explore realities, generate options, and commit to action. Developed by Sir John Whitmore.

The Four Stages

StageFocusKey Questions
GoalWhat do you want?What does success look like? How will you measure progress?
RealityWhere are you now?What's happening right now? What's helping or hindering you?
OptionsWhat could you do?What else? What are the pros/cons of each option?
Way ForwardWhat will you do?What's one step this week? How committed are you (1‚Äì10)?

When to Use It

Client ChallengeHow GROW Helps
"I don't know where to start."Clarifies outcomes and direction (Goal)
"I feel stuck."Unpacks what's really happening now (Reality)
"I don't know my options."Generates creative solutions (Options)
"I set goals but don't follow through."Builds commitment and action plans (Way Forward)

Limitations

  • Can feel too linear ‚Äî real conversations may not follow a clean four-step flow
  • Goal focus may miss deeper mindset or identity-level blockers
  • Assumes clarity of goals ‚Äî not all clients have a clear outcome at the start

The SCARF Model
Team dynamics ¬∑ Psychological safety ¬∑ Motivation
Intermediate

A neuroscience-based model by David Rock identifying five social domains that trigger threat or reward responses in the brain. Foundational for understanding team dynamics and psychological safety.

The Five Domains

DomainThreatReward
StatusFeeling undervalued or compared negativelyRecognition, respect, appreciation
CertaintyUnclear expectations, unpredictable changeClear goals, structure, transparency
AutonomyMicromanagement, no decision-making powerFreedom to choose, ownership of work
RelatednessFeeling isolated or excludedTrust, connection, belonging
FairnessPerceived favoritism, unfair treatmentEquitable, transparent decision-making

Common Coaching Prompts

  • "Which of the five SCARF domains feel most threatened for you right now?"
  • "What would increase your sense of certainty in this situation?"
  • "How might your team members be experiencing this change through SCARF?"

When to Use It

  • "I feel micromanaged." ‚Üí Autonomy threat
  • "This change feels sudden and unclear." ‚Üí Certainty gap
  • "I don't feel recognized for my work." ‚Üí Status threat

Ikigai
Purpose ¬∑ Career alignment ¬∑ Values exploration
Beginner-Friendly

A Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." Helps clients find the intersection of what they love, what they're good at, what the world needs, and what they can be paid for.

The Four Intersections

  • What you love (passion)
  • What you're good at (vocation)
  • What the world needs (mission)
  • What you can be paid for (profession)

Key Coaching Prompts

  • "When do you feel most alive or fulfilled?"
  • "What do people often ask you for help with?"
  • "What problem in the world do you care most about solving?"
  • "What would shift if your calendar reflected your Ikigai?"

Limitations

  • The Venn diagram is a Westernized adaptation ‚Äî may oversimplify the original concept
  • May feel idealistic or pressure-inducing for some clients
  • Requires time and reflection ‚Äî Ikigai is an evolving process, not a one-time exercise

The Ladder of Inference
Decision-making ¬∑ Assumptions ¬∑ Critical thinking
Advanced

Developed by Chris Argyris, this model shows how we move from observable data to beliefs and actions ‚Äî often too quickly and without awareness. Helps leaders slow down and examine their assumptions.

The Rungs (Bottom to Top)

1
Observable data ‚Äî What actually happened (facts)
2
Selected data ‚Äî What we choose to pay attention to
3
Interpreted meaning ‚Äî What we think it means
4
Assumptions ‚Äî What we assume based on interpretation
5
Conclusions ‚Äî What we decide is true
6
Beliefs ‚Äî What shapes our worldview
7
Actions ‚Äî What we do based on those beliefs

Key Prompts

  • "What did you actually observe ‚Äî just the facts?"
  • "What meaning did you assign to that? Where does that come from?"
  • "What might you be missing by focusing on this data?"
  • "What would someone else conclude from the same situation?"

Appreciative Inquiry

Intermediate

A strengths-based approach to change that focuses on what's already working, rather than problems to fix. Follows the 4-D cycle.

The Power of Habit Loop
Behavior change ¬∑ Routines ¬∑ Productivity
Beginner-Friendly

Based on Charles Duhigg's research, the Habit Loop reveals how habits form and how they can be changed. Three-part cycle: Cue ‚Üí Routine ‚Üí Reward.

The Three Parts

  • Cue ‚Äî The trigger that initiates the behavior (time, place, emotion, people)
  • Routine ‚Äî The behavior itself (mental, physical, or emotional)
  • Reward ‚Äî What the brain gets from the behavior (pleasure, relief, recognition)

Changing a Habit

Keep the cue and reward, but change the routine. Help clients identify what reward the current habit is actually serving, then find a healthier routine that delivers the same reward.

Key Prompts

  • "What triggers this behavior for you?"
  • "What do you get from it ‚Äî even if it's not working?"
  • "What new routine could give you the same reward?"

Cancellation & Late Cancel Policy
12-hour window ¬∑ Self-pay ¬∑ Coach compensation
Policy
Quick Reference
Late cancel = canceled within 12 hours of scheduled time ¬∑ Coaches are compensated for late cancels ¬∑ Late cancels count toward the client's session allocation ¬∑ Self-pay sessions: $80/session

What Counts as a Late Cancellation?

Any session canceled within 12 hours of the scheduled start time. Coaches are under no obligation to accept reschedule requests within the 12-hour window. If you choose to reschedule, that's at your discretion.

How to Handle a Late Cancel

1
You'll receive a cancellation email. The session should auto-label as "Late Cancel" in the Coach Portal.
2
If a client emails you to cancel within the window, log in manually and mark it as "Late Cancel" in the Coach Portal to ensure compensation.
3
Consider reaching out to check in with the client and maintain the relationship.

Guidance: Discussing Session Length & Frequency
How to handle client requests for longer or more frequent sessions
Guidance

Key Principles

  • Present options, not recommendations. Frame longer or more frequent sessions as options, not prescriptions. Example: "Boon offers options for longer or more frequent sessions, which can be paid for by the client or approved by their HR team."
  • Avoid "up-sell" language. Keep tone neutral. Instead of "I highly recommend a 45-minute session," say: "Some clients find longer sessions helpful for in-depth discussions ‚Äî this might be something you'd like to consider."
  • Check approval requirements. HR must provide written approval for changes to the standard arrangement. Have HR email approval to hello@boon-health.com.
  • Recommend 12 sessions. Please recommend scheduling 12 sessions for deeper engagement and a more seamless coaching journey.

Non-Emergency: Therapy Referral Process
When a client needs mental health support but is not in crisis
Protocol

During the Session ‚Äî How to Refer

  • Be direct and calm
  • Review limits of care as discussed in the first coaching session
  • Express concern and care
  • Point out specific behaviors that are concerning
  • Listen closely and encourage the client to seek help
  • Suggest they contact their health insurance provider for a covered therapist
  • Suggest they explore their company's EAP (email coachsupport@boon-health.com to ask about company-specific offerings)
  • Refer them to Mental Health America or SAMHSA's Find Treatment
  • Normalize therapy

When to Refer: Signs That Go Beyond Coaching
Symptoms that indicate a need for mental health referral
Clinical Guidance
Important Note
The appearance of any one symptom alone (except for active suicidality) does not necessitate immediate referral. When several symptoms appear together and the client is not presenting as their typical self, a referral must be made.

Symptoms to watch for include: marked changes in mood or behavior, expressions of hopelessness, references to self-harm, significant withdrawal from relationships or activities, paranoia or disconnection from reality, and inability to function in daily life.

Exception: Active suicidal ideation or any life-threatening situation requires immediate action regardless of other symptoms. See the Emergency Protocol above.
