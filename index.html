For GROW Clients

GROW clients are typically people leaders seeking to strengthen leadership skills, navigate team dynamics, and prepare for broader impact. Early sessions should balance immediate goals with deeper reflection.

The GROW Model
Goal setting ¬∑ Problem solving ¬∑ Coaching structure
Beginner-Friendly

The GROW Model is a structured coaching and problem-solving framework that helps individuals set goals, explore realities, generate options, and commit to action. Developed by Sir John Whitmore, it fosters clarity, accountability, and progress in personal and professional development.

The Four Stages

StageFocusExample Questions
GoalWhat do you want?"What do you want to achieve? What does success look like? How will you measure progress?"
RealityWhere are you now?"What is happening right now? What challenges are you facing? What strengths can you leverage?"
OptionsWhat could you do?"What are all the possible ways forward? What has worked in similar situations? What resources can help?"
Way ForwardWhat will you do?"What actions will you take? What obstacles might arise? How will you track progress?"

Real-World Use Cases

Client ChallengeHow GROW Helps

"I feel stuck or unclear on my progress."Unpacks what's really happening now (Reality)
"I don't know what my options are."Generates creative solutions and strategies (Options)
"I set goals but don't follow through."Builds commitment and action plans (Way Forward)
"I avoid accountability conversations."Offers a nonjudgmental, empowering structure for dialogue

Session Template

üìã GROW Coaching Template

Reality: What is happening right now? What's helping or hindering you? What have you already tried?

Options: What could you do? What else? And what else? What are the pros/cons of each option?

Way Forward: What will you do first? When will you do it? What support or resources do you need?

Ikigai
Purpose ¬∑ Career alignment ¬∑ Values exploration
Beginner-Friendly

Ikigai (Áîü„Åç„Åå„ÅÑ) is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." Individuals find deep purpose, motivation, and fulfillment when four areas align: what they love, what they're good at, what the world needs, and what they can be paid for.

The Four Intersections

  • What you love ‚Äî Passion: activities, causes, and interests that energize you
  • What you're good at ‚Äî Vocation: skills, strengths, and natural talents
  • What the world needs ‚Äî Mission: problems, opportunities, and communities you care about
  • What you can be paid for ‚Äî Profession: roles, services, or markets where your skills have value

How to Use It in a Session

  • Self-Discovery Exercise: Have clients reflect on and list answers to the four Ikigai questions. Encourage them to explore past experiences, strengths, and intrinsic motivations.
  • Identifying Gaps: If a client enjoys something but isn't paid for it, discuss ways to monetize it. If they are skilled but lack passion, explore ways to rekindle interest or pivot their skills toward meaningful work.
  • Career & Life Decisions: Use Ikigai to help clients navigate career transitions, entrepreneurship, or retirement. Assist in setting aligned goals that balance fulfillment with professional success.
  • Building Action Plans: Encourage small, actionable steps to bring more Ikigai into daily life. Integrate purpose-driven practices like gratitude, mindfulness, and continuous learning.

The Power of Habit Loop
Behavior change ¬∑ Routines ¬∑ Productivity
Beginner-Friendly

The Habit Loop, introduced by Charles Duhigg in The Power of Habit (2012), explains how habits form and can be changed. It consists of three key components: Cue ‚Üí Routine ‚Üí Reward. Understanding this cycle allows clients to modify behaviors and replace unproductive habits with beneficial ones.

The Three Components

  • Cue (Trigger): The event or signal that initiates a habit ‚Äî could be a time, location, emotion, person, or preceding action
  • Routine: The behavior or action taken in response to the cue ‚Äî mental, physical, or emotional
  • Reward: The benefit or satisfaction received, which reinforces the habit loop

How to Use It in a Session

1
Identify the Habit Loop: Break down a habit into its three components. Ask: What triggers this habit? What action follows automatically? What reward is reinforcing the behavior? Encourage clients to track patterns over a few days.
2
Disrupt Unwanted Habits: Modify the routine while keeping the same cue and reward. Example: If stress triggers mindless scrolling (routine) for relaxation (reward), replace scrolling with deep breathing ‚Äî same reward, healthier routine.
3
Build New Positive Habits: Attach new habits to existing cues (habit stacking). Ensure the reward is satisfying. Start small and build consistency.
4
Reinforce & Track Progress: Celebrate small wins and adjust as needed. Regular reflection on habit success and obstacles ensures long-term sustainability.

Real-World Use Cases

"I keep procrastinating on hard tasks."Identifies cue (discomfort), routine (avoidance), and reward (relief) to design a better response
"I start my day with good intentions but lose focus."Audits morning habits and creates a routine that aligns with values
"I want to build healthier communication habits."Unpacks social cues and emotional responses to replace reactions with intentional behaviors
"I want to meditate/work out/write, but I can't stick with it."Uses habit stacking and reward reinforcement to lock in new behavior
"I check email or Slack obsessively."Reveals unconscious habit loops driven by distraction and dopamine

Levels of Listening
Presence ¬∑ Coaching skill ¬∑ Leadership communication
Beginner-Friendly

The Levels of Listening framework helps individuals become more aware of how they listen and how it affects trust, connection, and understanding. Popularized by the Co-Active Training Institute and grounded in active listening research, this model is widely used in leadership development, coaching, and communication training.

The Three (or Four) Levels

LevelDescriptionExample
Level 1 ‚Äî InternalAttention is on your own thoughts, opinions, or how the conversation affects you. Often results in responding rather than truly understanding.Thinking about your own response while the other person is talking
Level 2 ‚Äî FocusedFull attention is on the speaker, with curiosity and openness. Includes paraphrasing, summarizing, and asking clarifying questions.A coach listening carefully and asking, "Can you say more about that?"
Level 3 ‚Äî GlobalAwareness extends beyond words to tone, emotions, and body language. The listener senses underlying emotions and unspoken messages.Recognizing when someone says "I'm fine" but their voice suggests sadness
Level 4 ‚Äî GenerativeListening so deep that it fosters transformative insights and co-creation. (Some models include this as an advanced fourth level.)A breakthrough insight emerges that neither person had before the conversation

How to Improve Listening Skills

  • Minimize Distractions: Put away devices and focus entirely on the conversation
  • Use Reflective Statements: "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated about this change."
  • Pay Attention to Nonverbal Cues: Observe body language, facial expressions, and tone of voice
  • Ask Open-Ended Questions: "What else is important about this?"
  • Hold Space for Silence: Allow pauses for deeper reflection and thought

Time Audit
Productivity ¬∑ Priorities ¬∑ Work-life balance
Beginner-Friendly

A Time Audit is a structured exercise that helps clients assess how they spend their time, identify inefficiencies, and make intentional adjustments to align daily activities with their goals. Particularly effective for clients struggling with time management, work-life balance, or productivity roadblocks.

How to Run a Time Audit

1
Define the Audit Period: Have the client select a tracking period (typically 3‚Äì7 days). Encourage them to track a typical week, not an unusually busy or slow one.
2
Record Activities: Log activities in 15‚Äì30 minute increments. Capture: the activity, duration, energy level and focus, and whether the task was necessary, productive, or a time-waster.
3
Categorize & Analyze: Group activities into buckets: deep work, admin tasks, collaboration, personal development, necessary tasks, distractions. Reflect: Where is time well spent? What takes longer than expected? What inefficiencies exist?
4
Make Adjustments: Reduce or eliminate time-wasters. Batch similar tasks. Delegate or automate low-value tasks. Schedule high-priority work during peak productivity hours.
5
Implement & Review: Test new strategies and periodically reevaluate. Adjustments should be based on ongoing reflections and evolving priorities.

Real-World Use Cases

"I feel burned out and stretched too thin."Helps identify energy drains and opportunities for recovery

"I'm doing too much tactical work."Exposes imbalances between strategic vs. executional tasks

The Eisenhower Matrix
Prioritization ¬∑ Time management ¬∑ Productivity
Beginner-Friendly

Also known as the Urgent-Important Matrix, the Eisenhower Matrix helps clients prioritize tasks based on urgency and importance. Inspired by President Dwight D. Eisenhower's decision-making principles and later popularized by Stephen Covey, it helps clients shift from reactive to proactive decision-making.

The Four Quadrants

QuadrantActionDescription
Q1: Urgent + ImportantDo FirstTasks that require immediate attention and have significant consequences if delayed ‚Äî crises, deadlines, pressing problems
Q2: Important, Not UrgentScheduleTasks that contribute to long-term goals but don't require immediate action ‚Äî strategic planning, skill development, relationship-building
Q3: Urgent, Not ImportantDelegateTasks that need to be done quickly but can be handled by someone else ‚Äî interruptions, routine requests, low-value meetings
Q4: NeitherEliminateDistractions or time-wasters that don't contribute to goals ‚Äî busywork, excessive social media, time-wasters

How to Use It in a Session

1
Identify & Categorize Tasks: Ask clients to list their current tasks, then categorize each into one of the four quadrants. Encourage honest reflection on what truly matters.
2
Focus on Quadrant 2: Many clients spend excessive time on urgent matters while neglecting long-term priorities. Help them schedule time for Q2 work (strategic planning, skill development) to prevent future crises.
3
Reduce Q3 & Q4: Guide clients in delegating non-essential urgent tasks (Q3). Encourage eliminating Q4 distractions.
4
Implement & Review: Support clients in executing their prioritized plan. Regularly revisit the matrix to reinforce productive habits.

Real-World Use Cases

Client ChallengeHow the Eisenhower Matrix Helps
"Everything feels urgent."Distinguishes between true urgency and reactive busyness
"I don't know what to focus on first."Clarifies priorities based on strategic value
"I spend all day in meetings or firefighting."Encourages boundary-setting and time blocking
"I procrastinate on big projects."Helps surface important-but-not-urgent work for scheduling
"I take on too much."Reveals tasks that can be delegated or deleted

Coaching Prompts

  • "Which quadrant do you spend most of your time in?"
  • "What percentage of your week is spent in Quadrant 2 work?"
  • "What makes something feel urgent when it might not be?"
  • "Where are you saying yes to things that belong in Q3 or Q4?"
  • "What would you need to protect in order to do more Q2 work?"

Cancellation & Late Cancel Policy
12-hour window ¬∑ Self-pay ¬∑ Coach compensation
Policy
Quick Reference
Late cancel = canceled within 12 hours of scheduled time ¬∑ Coaches are compensated for late cancels ¬∑ Late cancels count toward the client's session allocation ¬∑ Self-pay sessions: $80/session

What Counts as a Late Cancellation?

Any session canceled within 12 hours of the scheduled start time. Coaches are under no obligation to accept reschedule requests within the 12-hour window. If you choose to reschedule, that's at your discretion.

How to Handle a Late Cancel

1
You'll receive a cancellation email. The session should auto-label as "Late Cancel" in the Coach Portal.
2
If a client emails you to cancel within the window, log in manually and mark it as "Late Cancel" in the Coach Portal to ensure compensation.
3
Consider reaching out to check in with the client and maintain the relationship.

Guidance: Discussing Session Length & Frequency
How to handle client requests for longer or more frequent sessions
Guidance

Key Principles

  • Present options, not recommendations. Frame longer or more frequent sessions as options, not prescriptions. Example: "Boon offers options for longer or more frequent sessions, which can be paid for by the client or approved by their HR team."
  • Avoid "up-sell" language. Keep tone neutral. Instead of "I highly recommend a 45-minute session," say: "Some clients find longer sessions helpful for in-depth discussions ‚Äî this might be something you'd like to consider."
  • Check approval requirements. HR must provide written approval for changes to the standard arrangement. Have HR email approval to [email protected].
  • Recommend 12 sessions. Please recommend scheduling 12 sessions for deeper engagement and a more seamless coaching journey.

When to Refer: Signs That Go Beyond Coaching
Symptoms that indicate a need for mental health referral
Clinical Guidance
Important Note
The appearance of any one symptom alone (except for active suicidality) does not necessitate immediate referral. When several symptoms appear together and the client is not presenting as their typical self, a referral must be made.

Symptoms to watch for include: marked changes in mood or behavior, expressions of hopelessness, references to self-harm, significant withdrawal from relationships or activities, paranoia or disconnection from reality, and inability to function in daily life.

Exception: Active suicidal ideation or any life-threatening situation requires immediate action regardless of other symptoms. See the Emergency Protocol above.
